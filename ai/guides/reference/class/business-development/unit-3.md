## What is a System?

> "A System is an interconnected set of elements that is coherently organized in a way that achieves something"

A system consists of three core components:

- **Elements** -- the individual parts or actors within the system
- **Connections** -- the relationships and interactions between elements
- **Goal or Purpose** -- the function or outcome the system achieves

(Background diagram showing a complex system of interconnected nodes, pipes, valves, and flows.)

---

## Anatomy of a System -- Stocks & Flows

- **Stocks** -- the memory of the history of changing flows in the system (accumulations that can be measured at a point in time)

### Stocks & Flows Diagram (Fishing Example)

```
Flow IN: Reproduction Rate
        |
        v
Stock: Fish Population
        |
        v
Flow OUT: Fishing Rate
        |
        v
Stock: Caught Fish
```

- The **stock** is the fish population in the lake
- The **flow in** is the reproduction rate (fish being born)
- The **flow out** is the fishing rate (fish being caught)
- **Caught Fish** is a second stock that accumulates as fish are removed from the lake

---

## Anatomy of a System -- Feedback Loops

- How changes to the stock influence the flows that go back into the stock

### Reinforcing Loop (R): More leads to more

```
Investment --> Profit --> Reinvestment --> (back to Investment)
```

A reinforcing loop amplifies change. Investment generates profit, which is reinvested, leading to more investment and more profit -- a virtuous (or vicious) cycle.

### Balancing Loop (B): Pushes gap toward zero

```
Desired Temperature (Setpoint)
        |
        v
Gap = Desired - Current --> Thermostat / Controller --> Heater Output --> Current Room Temperature
        ^                                                                         |
        |_________________________________________________________________________|
```

A balancing loop seeks equilibrium. The thermostat measures the gap between the desired temperature and the current room temperature. If the gap is positive, the heater increases output. As the room warms, the gap shrinks, reducing heater output -- the system stabilizes toward the setpoint.

## System Examples -- Fishing Game (Basic)

A system diagram illustrating the fishing game activity:

**Stocks (accumulations):**
- Fish in Lake
- Fish in Warehouse

**Flows/Rates:**
- Catch Rate

**Elements and Connections:**
- **Fish in Lake** feeds into **Catch Rate**
- **Goal: Maximize Fish** drives **Catch Rate**
- **Fishfinder Added** increases **Catch Rate**
- **Catch Rate** produces **more momentum/effort** (reinforcing -- success breeds more effort)
- **Catch Rate** sends fish to **Fish in Warehouse**
- High catch rate **attracts more boats** which increases **Competition on Lake**
- **Competition on Lake** reduces **Catch Rate**

This demonstrates both reinforcing dynamics (success attracts more effort) and balancing dynamics (competition reduces individual catch rates).

## System Examples -- Fishing Game (Extended)

An expanded version of the fishing system diagram that adds competitive/strategic dynamics:

**Additional Stocks:**
- Your Fish in Warehouse
- Other Teams' Fish

**Additional Elements:**
- Relative Power / Leverage
- Steal Rate

**Extended Connections:**
- **Catch Rate** still feeds **Your Fish in Warehouse** with more momentum/effort
- **Catch Rate** still attracts more boats, increasing **Competition on Lake**, which reduces **Catch Rate**
- **Your Fish in Warehouse** compared to **Other Teams' Fish** determines **Relative Power / Leverage**
- **Relative Power / Leverage** enables a **Steal Rate** (ability to take from other teams)
- **Steal Rate** draws from **Other Teams' Fish**

This extended model shows how accumulation of resources (fish in warehouse) creates power dynamics and enables new strategic behaviors (stealing), adding another layer of system complexity beyond simple fishing.

## System Traps

Recurring structural patterns that produce undesirable outcomes:

- **Bounded Rationality** -- actors make locally rational decisions that are globally irrational because they can only see part of the system
- **Rule Beating** -- finding ways to comply with the letter of a rule while violating its spirit or intent
- **Success to the Successful** -- those who succeed gain advantages that make future success more likely, creating widening inequality
- **Tragedy of the Commons** -- individuals acting in rational self-interest deplete a shared resource, leading to collective collapse

## Don't Blame People

- **Systems Shape Behavior** -- the structure of the system drives the behavior of the people within it, not the other way around

> "For if you suffer your people to be ill-educated, and their manners to be corrupted from their infancy, and then punish them for those crimes to which their first education disposed them, what else is to be concluded from this, but that you first make thieves and then punish them"

(This quote emphasizes that systemic conditions -- poor education, corrupted upbringing -- create the behaviors we then blame individuals for. The system must be fixed, not just the people.)

## Leverage Points: Interacting with & Changing a System

Places within a system where a small intervention can produce outsized effects. Organized from least to most impactful:

### Surface-level leverage
- **Parameters** -- adjusting numbers, constants, and settings
- **Thresholds** -- changing trigger points and limits
- **Numbers** -- modifying quantities

### Structural leverage
- **Feedback loops** -- altering or adding reinforcing/balancing feedback mechanisms
- **Incentives** -- changing what behaviors are rewarded or penalized
- **Information flows** -- changing who has access to what information and when

### Deep leverage
- **Goals** -- redefining what the system is trying to achieve
- **Rules** -- changing the fundamental rules that govern behavior within the system
- **Assumptions / paradigms** -- challenging and shifting the underlying mental models and worldviews that the system is built upon

(Illustration of a lever and fulcrum: small effort on one side produces big impact on the other side, with "LEVERAGE POINT" at the fulcrum.)

## System Examples -- Hiring (Basic)

A detailed system diagram modeling the entry-level hiring system:

**External Forces (Drivers):**
- AI Capability Growth --> Task Automation --> Market Pressure
- Projects, Portfolios, Referrals (signal-building activities)

**Stocks:**
- Stock: Entry Level Applicants
- Stock: Demonstrated Skill Signal
- Stock: Experience Gap
- Stock: Employer Trust
- Stock: Open Entry Level Roles

**Flows/Rates:**
- Signal Building Rate (how fast applicants build demonstrated skill signals)
- Skill Obsolescence Rate (how fast skills become outdated)
- Application Rate
- Hiring Rate

**Key Connections:**
- **AI Capability Growth** leads to **Task Automation**, which creates **Market Pressure**
- **Market Pressure** reduces **Application Rate** (fewer roles, less motivation to apply)
- **Application Rate** feeds into **Stock: Open Entry Level Roles**
- **Projects, Portfolios, Referrals** drive **Signal Building Rate**, which builds **Stock: Demonstrated Skill Signal**
- **Demonstrated Skill Signal** provides **visible proof** to **Automated Screening**
- **Automated Screening** increases competition and raises the bar for **Hiring Rate**
- **Skill Obsolescence Rate** reduces **Stock: Entry Level Applicants**
- Lack of experience increases **Stock: Experience Gap**, which blocks **Hiring Rate**
- **Stock: Employer Trust** unlocks **Hiring Rate**
- **Experience Gap** reduces **Employer Trust**

## System Examples -- Hiring (with Leverage Points)

The same hiring system diagram with identified leverage points labeled:

- **LP1: Apply more** -- surface-level leverage point at the Market Pressure / Application Rate junction (increase volume of applications)
- **LP2: Resume and credentials** -- leverage point at the Application Rate / Automated Screening junction (improve traditional credentials to pass screening)
- **LP3: Reduce experience gap** -- leverage point targeting the Stock: Experience Gap directly (gain experience through alternative means)
- **LP4: Build visible skill** -- leverage point at the Stock: Entry Level Applicants / Demonstrated Skill Signal junction (create tangible proof of ability)
- **LP5: Direct outreach** -- leverage point feeding into Projects, Portfolios, Referrals (bypass automated systems through personal connections)
- **LP6: Optimize for evidence not permission** -- leverage point at the Stock: Entry Level Applicants level (shift paradigm from waiting for permission to demonstrating capability)

These leverage points range from surface-level (LP1: just apply more) to deep leverage (LP6: fundamentally change the approach from seeking permission to creating evidence).

## Why This Matters for IS Students

- **IS or CS?** -- framing the distinction between Information Systems and Computer Science
- **AI Has Changed the Terrain**
    - Codifiable work gets cheaper (AI can do routine coding/execution tasks)
    - Judgement and framing remain scarce (understanding problems, making decisions)
- **Information flow is leverage** -- IS students are uniquely positioned because controlling and designing information flows is one of the most powerful structural leverage points in any system
- **Orchestration is greater than execution** -- the ability to coordinate and design systems (orchestration) is more valuable than the ability to perform individual tasks (execution)
