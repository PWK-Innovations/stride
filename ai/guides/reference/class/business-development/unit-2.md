## Is It a Failure, or a Pattern?

- **Confidence Comes Early** -- we form strong beliefs quickly, often before we have adequate evidence
- **Evidence Comes Later** -- the data that would confirm or deny our beliefs arrives after we have already committed to a direction

This is not an occasional failure; it is a systematic, repeatable pattern in human cognition.

## Why This Gets Expensive

When thinking fails in a business context, the costs compound across three dimensions:

- **Time** -- pursuing the wrong direction wastes time that cannot be recovered
- **Resources** -- money, people, and effort are spent on flawed plans
- **Momentum** -- once a team or organization is moving in a direction, it becomes increasingly difficult to change course

---

## Confidence Hardens Paths

**Quick Exercise -- Divergent + Convergent Thinking**

Once confidence sets in, it narrows our thinking. This exercise explores two modes:

- **Divergent thinking** -- generating many possible ideas, directions, or explanations
- **Convergent thinking** -- narrowing down to one best answer

The point is that confidence causes us to skip divergent thinking and jump straight to convergent thinking, locking us into a path prematurely.

---

## Why AI Makes This Worse

> AI reduces the cost of being confidently wrong, right up until it suddenly doesn't.

AI tools can accelerate execution on a plan, making it cheaper and faster to build on your assumptions. This feels productive, but if the underlying assumptions are wrong, AI just helps you go further down the wrong path faster. The moment the flawed assumption surfaces, the accumulated cost hits all at once.

## Why Does This Happen?

Many reasons exist, but the lecture focuses on three primary causes:

1. Prediction Failure
2. Elephants & Riders
3. Information & Interpretation

---

## Reason 1 -- Prediction Failure

**Discussion Prompt:** Talk to your neighbor!

> When a top-tier financial analyst with a history of success makes predictions about the future, would you trust them? Why or why not?

The key insight is that humans are systematically bad at forecasting outcomes, especially in complex or novel domains. Past success does not guarantee future prediction accuracy. Expertise in stable environments does not transfer well to uncertain, changing environments -- precisely the environments where startups and new products operate.

---

## Reason 2 -- Elephants & Riders

This is a framework for understanding the two systems of human decision-making:

- **The Elephant** -- intuition, emotion, gut feeling. This is the powerful force that actually drives most of our decisions.
- **The Rider** -- rational, analytical thinking. The rider sits on top of the elephant and *thinks* it is in control, but mostly it is rationalizing the direction the elephant has already chosen.

The rider's primary job in practice is not to steer the elephant but to construct post-hoc justifications for the elephant's choices. Effective decision-making requires understanding which system is actually in control at any given moment.

Intuition (the elephant) works well in stable, repetitive environments where patterns are reliable. It breaks down in novel situations where past patterns do not apply.

---

## Reason 3 -- Information & Interpretation

> When confident decisions go wrong, is it usually because people lacked information, or because they misread what they had?

The distinction is critical:

- **Information gap** -- the data was not available
- **Interpretation failure** -- the data was there, but it was read incorrectly

Most confident failures are interpretation failures, not information gaps. People had access to the relevant data but filtered, framed, or ignored it in ways that supported their existing beliefs.

## How Do We Fix It?

The lecture transitions from diagnosing the problem to providing actionable tools.

---

## Falsifiability

> What would need to be true in order for this to be false? How does this fail?

**It's a null hypothesis.**

Falsifiability is a critical thinking tool borrowed from the scientific method. A belief or hypothesis only has value if it can be proven wrong. The discipline is to ask, before committing to a direction: *What evidence would convince me this is wrong?*

If you cannot define what failure looks like, you cannot meaningfully test your idea. Unfalsifiable claims are not useful for making product or business decisions.

---

## Set Falsifiable Guardrails Early

- **What could we rationalize away?** -- identify the specific results or signals that you might be tempted to explain away if they contradict your hypothesis
- **Unless we decide in advance what counts as failure, confidence will protect us** -- without pre-committed criteria, our natural tendency is to reinterpret negative results as noise or special cases rather than genuine disconfirmation
- **Theranos** -- used as an example of what happens when falsifiable guardrails are absent. The company maintained confidence in its vision long past the point where evidence should have forced a reckoning, because no one had defined in advance what failure would look like

---

## Quick And Small At The Beginning Is Cheaper

- **Cheap tests** -- run small, low-cost experiments before committing significant resources. The goal is to expose flawed assumptions early, when the cost of being wrong is still low.
- **Reversible bets** -- prefer decisions that can be undone or adjusted. Avoid making large, irreversible commitments based on untested confidence.

---

## The Bigger Picture

**Confidence does not equal Commitment**

The concluding principle: being confident in an idea does not mean you should be fully committed to it. Confidence is a feeling; commitment is a resource allocation. You can hold an idea confidently while still subjecting it to rigorous testing before investing heavily.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Intelligence is not a shield.** Smart people are often *better* at rationalizing flawed conclusions, not better at avoiding them.
2. **Prediction failure is systematic.** Humans are reliably bad at forecasting, especially in novel or complex domains.
3. **The elephant drives the rider.** Intuition makes most decisions; rationality mostly justifies them after the fact.
4. **Interpretation fails more than information.** Bad decisions usually come from misreading available data, not from lacking it.
5. **Confidence comes before evidence.** We form strong beliefs quickly and then seek confirming data.
6. **AI amplifies the problem.** AI reduces the cost of executing on wrong assumptions, making the eventual reckoning more expensive.
7. **Falsifiability is the antidote.** Define what failure looks like *before* you commit. Ask: "What would need to be true for this to be false?"
8. **Set guardrails early.** Decide in advance what counts as failure so confidence cannot rationalize away negative signals.
9. **Start quick and small.** Use cheap tests and reversible bets to expose bad assumptions before they become expensive.
10. **Confidence does not equal commitment.** Hold ideas confidently but test them rigorously before making irreversible resource decisions.
